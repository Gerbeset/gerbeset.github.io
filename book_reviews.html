Foundryside by Robert Jackson Bennett

Category: Fantasy

Rating: 8.5/10

This book surprised me. Calling it "magic" is a bit of a misnomer. A better description would be magic-based sci-fi. The premise is following: objects can be altered by writing on them (called scriving) which changes their reality. For example: someone could take a lumber beam, scrive it with sigils that tell it it's made of iron, and then the beam now behaves as if it was made of iron. While the foundation of the story is a kind of magic, its functionality is sci-fi. These sigils can be hacked: certain characters can "talk" to scrived objects, listen to the logic of their sigils, then twist the meaning to make the scrived objects do something they weren't meant to. As a programmer, this plot device was immensely interesting. People who know how to write these sigils are called scrivers, and they essentially are like computer programmers: they think about how to combine different sigils and "words" to have a desired effects. They have massive objects called lexicons to define new sigils, but it only has a limited range of effectiveness. It's like having a library of functions in python or C++! One thing this book does remarkably well is build the world through plot. One of my major pet-peeves with fantasy novels is building the world through exposition dumps. It feels like reading a textbook, and I read books to avoid scholastic activities. But Foundryside does an excellent job of showing the reader the world through the plot: the magic is explained by exploring its impacts in the world. We learn of the ability to hack scrived objects because the main character needs to break a lock. We learn about the lore through its influence on characters decisions and motivations. The main character was "scrived." It reminds the reader of contemporary concerns like micro-chipping human minds. The actually writing was innovative. The way characters talk with scrived devices was entertaining and engaging. The way some objects were loud and obnoxious while others have charisma and personality added a depth to the scriving mechanic that I was not expecting. In short: the world building was fresh and innovative. The magic was constructed in such a way to not be magic in the traditional sense, but to function more in a technological way. It allows the characters to live in a world with rapiers, crossbows, and fortresses, but wrestle with issues that echo our very real questions regarding AI, coding, hacking, and bio-tech. The characters are engaging, though a bit derivative at times.
